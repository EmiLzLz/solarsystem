ABOUT MERCURY

It is the closest to the Sun; it revolves around it at an average speed of 170,505 km/h and takes only 88 days to complete one revolution, so that each year of the Earth is equivalent to more than four years of Mercury. Because of its proximity to our star, from the surface of Mercury our Sun would be seen 3 times larger than from Earth, and its brightness would be up to 7 times higher. It was already known at least since the time of the Sumerians, 5,000 years ago.

WHY THAT NAME?

The Greeks named it Hermes, in honor of the messenger of the Olympian gods, who was later known by the Romans as Mercury, the name by which it has survived to the present day.

The name of this planet is no coincidence because, just as Mercury was the fastest of the gods of Olympus, with a speed of 170,5030 kilometers per hour, the planet Mercury is the one that travels the fastest through space in our solar system.

THE MOST INTERESTING FACT

Since Mercury has no atmosphere, the asteroids that reach the planet hit it without any resistance. The result: formation of large craters. An example of a crater resulting from an asteroid impact 4 billion years ago is the Caloris Basin. It is believed that the 100 km wide asteroid that caused the impact hit Mercury with a force similar to that produced by a trillion bombs of one megaton each.
