ABOUT NEPTUNE

Located 4300 million km from Earth at its closest point, Neptune is the farthest planet from the Sun, so it is not surprising that the great ice giant is an inhospitable wasteland. It receives hardly any light or heat, so its average temperature is about -200 °C. On Neptune, a year lasts 165 Earth years. Its long summers and 40-year winters allow a moderate warming of 10 °C at the poles.

WHY THAT NAME?

Shortly after its discovery, Neptune was called, simply, "the planet that follows Uranus" or "Le Verrier's planet".

While Le Verrier suggested Neptune for the new planet. Struve came out in favor of that name on December 29, 1846, at the St. Petersburg Academy of Sciences. In Roman mythology, Neptune was the god of the sea, identified with the Greek Poseidon. The demand for a mythological name seemed to be in keeping with the nomenclature of the other planets, which were all named after Roman deities.

THE MOST INTERESTING FACT

As is usually the case with large planets, Neptune has a large number of satellites. Experts have identified 14 moons, although the number could be higher as it has not been definitively closed.
Of these moons, Triton is the only one that is completely spherical in shape and is believed to have originally been a dwarf planet that eventually yielded to the pull of the icy giant's gravity field. It has been confirmed that Triton is a rocky and icy body, which makes it very similar to Pluto, and that it also has volcanic activity.
