ABOUT SATURN

Its main feature is its seven rings composed of ice and dust that have a length of thousands of kilometers, but an average thickness of only 10 m in some places. To the human eye, its "surface" appears to be formed by bands, an effect caused by its storms and extreme winds.

WHY THAT NAME?

Saturn is the last planet that was already known in antiquity. The ancient Greeks named it Chronos, after their god of time and agriculture, because it has the longest period of observation of all the planets.

It should be remembered that Cronus was considered the guardian of time. The Romans named him after their equivalent deity, Saturn (who also, like Cronus with Zeus, was the father of Jupiter).

THE MOST INTERESTING FACT

Saturn has a unique meteorological phenomenon in the solar system: a hexagonal jet stream over its north pole. Observations by the Voyager 1 spacecraft and later by Cassini have helped to establish its size: about 30,000 km wide, it harbors a powerful storm at its center and can reach speeds of 320 km/h (200 mph).
