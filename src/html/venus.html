ABOUT VENUS

It is the hottest planet in the solar system, with surface temperatures reaching 470 Â°C, but at about 48 km altitude, where clouds form, the temperature plummets to levels similar to those on the Earth's surface. The very high levels of carbon dioxide in its atmosphere, combined with the absence of surface water, mean that it cannot regulate its temperature, so it gets hotter and hotter.

WHY THAT NAME?

It is the third brightest celestial object in the sky (after the Sun and the Moon). It is even capable of casting shadows on the surface of our planet. Despite being a planet with hellish conditions, it was associated with the opposite.

Its brightness, and its appearance at dawn, led the ancient Romans to associate the planet with Venus, the goddess of love and beauty. They were not the only ones to be guided by such impressions, for the ancient Greeks associated it with its equivalent, Aphrodite.

THE MOST INTERESTING FACT

We know that Venus is a totally hostile planet for life, except for what we know as extremophile organisms, and its hurricane winds are a clear example of this.

Winds can reach up to 724 kilometers per hour. To give you an idea, the tornadoes that occur on Earth have average speeds of 180 kilometers per hour. In addition, Venus has been tilted so much that it is almost completely upside down, to a net tilt of less than three degrees to the sun, so the seasonal effect is negligible.
